Nelson Chang: Imported Cement Shows "Transfer Effect"; Government Must Address Unfair Competition

Hualien, September 27 (CNA) â€“ Nelson Chang, chairman of Taiwan Cement Corporation (TCC), pointed out that at the end of July this year, the Ministry of Finance's Customs Administration put anti-dumping taxes on Portland cement from Vietnam. However, starting in August, the amount of cement imported from Indonesia grew by more than 50%. This shows a "transfer effect." Taiwan is one of the few countries in the world that has no tariffs on imported cement. Only putting anti-dumping taxes on one country cannot solve the main problem. He asks the government to look at the unfair competition from imported cement, which has no tariffs and no carbon fees.

Today, Nelson Chang was in Hualien for the opening of TCC's Resource Recycling Center. After the event, he said that the Cement Manufacturers' Association and the government talked for a year and a half before the government decided to put anti-dumping taxes on Portland cement from Vietnam. But he believes that taking action against only one country will not completely solve the problem.

He explained that according to statistics from the Ministry of Finance's Customs Administration, the amount of cement and clinker imported into Taiwan in the first eight months of this year was 10% less from Vietnam compared to the same time last year. However, it grew by 54% from Indonesia. This shows that after the government put anti-dumping taxes on cement from Vietnam at the end of July this year, the source of imported cement changed. News from China Cement website reports that the Indonesian cement market includes 9 million tons from Conch Cement's investments and 7 million tons from Red Lion Cement's production.

Nelson Chang also pointed out that Taiwan is one of the few countries in the world that has no import tariffs on cement. Local companies have to pay carbon fees, but imported cement does not have this carbon cost. "The carbon from imported goods is counted in Taiwan, but we have to pay carbon fees for exported products. This is very unfair," he stated.

He said that rules for public construction projects require that cement used for the same project must come from the same place. This is to make sure the quality is constant and the project is safe. TCC's cement from its Heping and Su-ao plants can be traced back to its origin and follows these rules. In contrast, imported cement often comes from many different places and does not have a clear tracking system, yet a lot of it is still sold in the market.

Nelson Chang said that imported cement usually comes in large bags for concrete factories. When it is unloaded, it can create a lot of dust, which pollutes the area. If the government does not check and manage this more carefully, imported cement could cause problems with quality control and environmental protection. This would lead to unfair competition for local businesses.

TCC asks the government to require imported cement clinker to show its carbon emissions. They also ask the government to consider collecting a carbon payment or creating a Taiwan version of CBAM (Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism). This way, the hard work of local industries will not be wasted.

Also, when asked about the fire at Sanyuan Energy in Kaohsiung and the outlook for the fourth quarter, Nelson Chang only said that for Sanyuan Energy, they would have to wait for the fire investigation to finish before they could get insurance money. He did not want to say more. However, he emphasized that operations in Taiwan, mainland China, and Europe are stable, but the fire at Sanyuan Energy has greatly affected TCC's profits.
