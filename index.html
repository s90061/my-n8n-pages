Hong Kong, Tibet, Xinjiang Issues Sensitive: Thai Arts Centre Gives in to Chinese Pressure, Removes Exhibition Works

Reuters reported from Bangkok on August 7th that one of Thailand's main art galleries, the Bangkok Arts and Cultural Centre, changed or removed art pieces from an exhibition. These pieces were about how Beijing treats minority groups and Hong Kong. This happened because China asked them to. Artists said this is another time China has tried to stop criticism from outside its borders.

The exhibition at the Bangkok Arts and Cultural Centre was called "Constellation of Complicity: Visualising the Global Machinery of Authoritarian Solidarity." It looked at how strict governments in different countries work together across borders. The artworks were made by artists who had to leave their homes in places like Tibet, Xinjiang, Russia, Iran, and Syria.

Today, Reuters reporters went to the exhibition. They saw that some artworks that were shown in advertisements and photos before were gone. This included a multi-media art piece by an artist from Tibet. Other artworks were changed. Words like "Hong Kong," "Tibet," and "Uighur," or the names of the artists, were covered up.

Sai, an artist from Myanmar and one of the people who organized the exhibition, said that the show opened on July 24th. Three days later, people from the Chinese embassy, with officials from Bangkok City, came to the exhibition hall. They asked for the exhibition to be stopped.

Reuters saw an email from the Bangkok Arts and Cultural Centre dated July 30th. It said: "The Chinese embassy put pressure on us through the Thai Ministry of Foreign Affairs, and especially through the Bangkok City Government, which is our main supporter. We were warned that this exhibition could cause diplomatic problems between Thailand and China."

The Bangkok Arts and Cultural Centre also wrote in the email that they "had no choice but to make some changes." This included covering the names of artists from Hong Kong, Tibet, and the Uighur region.

Sai, the organizer, said that later, the Chinese embassy asked them to take down even more artworks. He said these artworks included the Tibetan snow lion flag, the East Turkestan flag of the Uighur people, several postcards showing Chinese President Xi Jinping, and one postcard about the relationship between China and Israel.

One of the removed artworks was by the Tibetan artist Tenzin Mingyur Paldron. It included a video where Tibetan people held the Palestinian flag and asked for people to be held responsible for genocide. Another video was called "Listen to Indigenous People."

Reuters reporters saw that the video player showed a black screen at the exhibition. Another video by a Uighur artist was still playing, but the artist's name was covered with black paint.

Sai said he has left Thailand because Thai police are looking for him. He also shared recent news from his contacts in Bangkok. They told him that Chinese officials returned to the Bangkok Arts and Cultural Centre yesterday. They asked for a flyer to be removed and repeated that they want Thailand to follow the "One China policy."

Sai said, "It is sad and ironic that an exhibition about how strict governments work together was censored because of pressure from a strict government. For a long time, Thailand has been a safe place for people who speak out against governments. But this event sends a scary message to all artists and activists who live in exile in this area."

Taipei Ranks First as World's Most Desired City to Live In, Beats Singapore in Survey

Taipei, August 8th â€“ Gensler, a global design and architecture company, recently released a report called "City Pulse 2025: The Magnetic City." In this report, Taipei was named the "world's most desired city to live in," beating other big international cities like Singapore, Sydney, and Berlin.

Gensler's global survey, "City Pulse 2025: The Magnetic City," collected opinions from 33,000 city residents in 65 cities around the world. It looked at why and where people choose to live.

The survey showed the percentage of people in each city who said they were "unlikely" to move. The top 10 international cities where people want to live long-term are: Taipei (64%), Ho Chi Minh City (61%), Singapore (59%), Sydney (58%), Berlin (51%), Monterrey (50%), Munich (49%), Sao Paulo (49%), Vancouver (49%), and Seoul (47%).

According to Gensler's survey, out of 152 factors, the top 5 reasons why people choose to live in a city for a long time are: "I don't get bored in this city," "I feel at home in this city," "I am proud of this city," "This city is becoming better for older people," and "My sense of belonging to the city grows over time."

In the United States, the top 10 cities where people want to live long-term are: Minneapolis (53%), San Antonio (53%), San Diego (50%), Raleigh (49%), Chicago (47%), Tampa (47%), Portland (47%), Phoenix (46%), San Francisco (46%), and Austin (46%).

Also, in this report, Taipei scored higher than the global average in several areas. These include economic strength (73%), safety and stability (78%), city excitement (76%), ability to handle climate change (69%), improving quality of life (71%), how much is invested in communities (70%), helping homeless people (60%), and reducing crime (71%).
