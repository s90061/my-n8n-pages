After the 726 Recall Votes, Taiwanese Groups Abroad Ask for Unity to Face Big Problems

(Washington D.C. July 29, CNA) After the big recall votes on July 26, five groups of Taiwanese people living in America and Europe asked everyone to come together again. They also said that Taiwan is talking with the United States about things like taxes on goods. If there is political trouble inside Taiwan, it will make Taiwan's position weaker in these talks. If opposing political parties use their efforts to cause problems in the parliament, it will not help people's lives and might even make the United States support Taiwan less.

On July 26, people voted on whether to remove 24 Kuomintang (KMT) lawmakers and Hsinchu City Mayor Kao Hung-an from their jobs. All these recall efforts failed. The North America Taiwanese Association, the Federation of Taiwanese Associations in Europe, the North American Taiwanese Women's Association, the Taiwanese Association in Manchester, UK, and the Greater Vancouver Taiwanese Association released a joint statement in the afternoon to share their views.

The statement said that the people of Taiwan showed their choices through a democratic process. Taiwan faces difficult situations in the world. The groups hope that after the voting, society can unite and protect democracy together.

"Please respect the results of democracy and value the trust given by voters," these five groups said. They stated that the recall actions showed the anger of citizen groups and their disappointment and distrust in how the opposition lawmakers handled things in parliament. These feelings should be noticed and thought about.

President Lai Ching-te and his government team are working according to the law and the constitution. They should be respected and given space to continue making changes inside the country and planning for international relations. They need to handle the risks and pressures for the country.

The North America Taiwanese Association and other groups also said that the real danger to Taiwan's democracy comes from the Chinese Communist Party's control and hidden influence. "Overseas, we strongly feel that the Chinese Communist Party uses many ways to threaten democratic countries."

According to the statement, the Chinese Communist Party continues to interfere in Taiwan's matters. They do this by controlling public opinion, spreading false information, and training political agents. They want to divide society, cause political trouble, and finally take over Taiwan, destroying its democracy.

The statement pointed out that Taiwan is having difficult talks with the United States about many things like customs taxes, trade, supply chains, and safety. Any political problems inside Taiwan will make Taiwan weaker in these international talks and make it less trusted.

The statement said that if the opposing political parties use all their political power to fight against the government and cause chaos in parliament, it will not only hurt people's daily lives but also might make the United States and other democratic countries lose trust and support for Taiwan.

The North America Taiwanese Association and other groups asked the parliament and all political parties to stop unnecessary political games. They should go back to focusing on public policies and people's lives. The government team should stay on a steady path, keep working to develop the country, and connect with other countries, not letting problems shake their beliefs.

At the same time, the public should be aware of attempts to influence their thoughts and beliefs, often through media. They should keep their ability to understand media well so they are not fooled by political talk and false information. Taiwanese people both inside and outside the country should support each other, talk calmly, and face dangers and problems from outside together.

Old Photos: Hu De-fu and the Movement to Give Indigenous People Their Rightful Name

(CNA website) On August 1, 1994, the word "Sh캐n B캐o" (mountain people), which was a disrespectful name, was finally removed from the Constitution. It was changed to "indigenous people." Three years later, it was changed again to "indigenous peoples," which is the full and correct name.

This was a movement that lasted for 10 years. On this journey, the name of Hu De-fu (Kimbo) cannot be forgotten.

He is from the Puyuma and Paiwan indigenous groups. In 1962, Hu De-fu traveled north from Taitung to study at Tamkang High School. He then became the first student from his hometown to get into the Foreign Languages and Literatures Department at National Taiwan University.

He had to leave university in his third year because of a brain injury that caused seizures. Later, to help his family, he started singing in cafes. While singing at a coffee center linked to the Colombian Embassy, he met a group of good friends, including Li Shuang-tze, Yang Hsien, and Wu Chu-chu.

In 1974, Hu De-fu held a concert called "Beautiful Rice Grains" with strong support from Li Shuang-tze. This concert marked the beginning of Taiwan's folk song movement and was a very important moment in his life.

Li Shuang-tze's words, "Sing your own songs, speak your own words," greatly influenced Hu De-fu. He began to find his people's traditional songs and also wrote his own music.

In 1984, the Haishan coal mine in Taipei County (now New Taipei City) had an explosion. When Hu De-fu helped with the rescue, he found that most of the people who died were Amis miners. After he went home, he wrote a song called "Why?". He then held a concert in New Park to speak out and support the indigenous people who suffered greatly in the disaster.

At the end of the same year, even though many police officers tried to stop him, he and Icyang Parod and others started the "Taiwan Indigenous Peoples' Rights Promotion Association" at the MacKay Memorial Hospital hall in Taipei. Hu De-fu was chosen as its first leader. He worked to get the indigenous people their rightful name and their land back.

On August 25, 1988, the first large social movement for indigenous peoples after martial law ended in Taiwan took place. More than 2,000 indigenous people marched in the streets, shouting, "We want our land, and we want our name!"

In 1994, then-President Lee Teng-hui first used the term "indigenous people" in public in Pingtung. In July of the same year, thanks to the hard work of National Assembly member Kao Cheng-chih, a change to the Constitution was passed by only one vote. It was officially announced on August 1. This day also became "Indigenous Peoples' Day."
