UK Foreign Secretary: China Is a Security Threat, Including Cross-Border Repression and Spying

British prosecutors recently said that a case accusing two men of spying for China would be dropped. This was because the government did not say China was a threat to national security. However, British Foreign Secretary Yvette Cooper said in an interview today that China does threaten the UK's safety.

The Daily Telegraph reported that the Labour Party won the election last July. But after they took power, they did not call China a threat. This is because the Labour government's foreign office promised to work with Beijing on things like healthcare and reaching net-zero emissions.

Cooper said on BBC Radio 4, "I want to be clear that we know China is a threat to UK national security. This includes stopping people from speaking freely in other countries, spying, and bad online actions. We have said this before."

She also added, "Of course, they are also a trade partner. They are also an important partner in dealing with climate change. But I am very upset about this case, because I certainly wanted to see it go to court."

Christopher Cash, 30, who used to be the director of the China Research Group in the UK Parliament, and Christopher Berry, 33, a teacher, were first arrested in March 2023. In April last year, they were accused of giving secret political information to Chinese intelligence officers. But both men said they were not guilty of any charges.

Reuters reported that British prosecutors had earlier accused these two men under the Official Secrets Act. This law says it is a crime to give any documents that might help an "enemy state" to that state.

But last month, British prosecutors suddenly dropped the charges against the two men. This was just a few weeks before they were supposed to go to trial.

A spokesperson for British Prime Minister Keir Starmer said on October 6 that the previous government called China an "epochal challenge." They never clearly said it was an enemy state.

The Conservative Party, which was in power until last year, and other opposition parties said that Starmer's Labour government intentionally stopped the trial. They did this to avoid making China angry.

British prosecutors said on October 7 that they had "done everything they could" to take the case to court. But in the end, the case could not go forward because the government refused to call China an enemy state.
