Old Photos Unboxed: Hu Te-fu and the Movement to Rename Indigenous Peoples

(CNA website) On August 1, 1994, the word "mountain compatriots," which was a disrespectful term, was finally removed from the Constitution. It was changed to "indigenous people." Three years later, it was changed again to "indigenous peoples."

This was a movement that lasted for 10 years. On this journey, the name Hu Te-fu (Kimbo) cannot be forgotten.

He is a descendant of the Puyuma and Paiwan tribes. In 1962, Hu Te-fu moved from Taitung to Taipei to study at Tamkang Middle School. He was also the first person from his hometown to get into the Foreign Languages Department at National Taiwan University.

He had to stop studying in his third year of university because of a brain injury that caused seizures. Later, to help his family with money, he started singing in cafes. While singing at a coffee center linked to the Colombian Embassy, he met good friends like Li Shuang-tze, Yang Hsien, and Wu Chu-chu.

In 1974, Hu Te-fu held a concert called "Beautiful Rice Grains," thanks to Li Shuang-tze's strong support. This concert started the folk song movement in Taiwan and was also a major turning point in Hu Te-fu's life.

"Sing your own songs, speak your own words." These words from Li Shuang-tze deeply influenced Hu Te-fu. He started to find his people's traditional songs again and also wrote his own music.

In 1984, there was a coal mine explosion in Taipei County (now New Taipei City) at the Haisan coal mine. Hu Te-fu helped with the rescue work and found that most of the people who died were Amis miners. After he went home, he wrote a song called "Why?" He also held a concert in New Park to speak out and support the indigenous people who suffered in the disaster.

At the end of the same year, even though many police tried to stop them, he and Icyang Parod and others started the "Taiwan Aborigines' Rights Promotion Association" in the auditorium of Mackay Memorial Hospital in Taipei. Hu Te-fu became the first president. They worked for the "Rectification of Name" and "Give Us Back Our Land" movements.

On August 25, 1988, the first big social movement for indigenous people happened after martial law ended in Taiwan. More than 2,000 indigenous people marched on the streets and shouted: "We want our land, and we want our names!"

In 1994, the President at that time, Lee Teng-hui, used the term "indigenous people" in public for the first time in Pingtung. In July of the same year, thanks to the efforts of National Assembly member Kao Cheng-chih, a change to the Constitution was passed by just one vote. It was officially announced on August 1. This day also became "Indigenous Peoples' Day."
