Taiwanese Woman Becomes Interpreter in Italy, Hopes to Share Culture

A Taiwanese woman with cool hair often works at big international meetings and business talks in Italy. She can speak Chinese, Italian, and English very well. Her name is Hsiao Nai-yen, and she has been a full-time interpreter in Italy for 10 years. For her, this job is more than just changing words from one language to another. It is also about sharing and connecting different cultures, which is what she has always wanted to do since she was a child.

Hsiao Nai-yen is 31 years old this year. She studied Italian at Fu Jen Catholic University. In 2015, she started working as a full-time interpreter in Italy. She told Central News Agency that she has not met any other full-time Taiwanese interpreters in Italy yet. This shows how special her job is.

Biggest Challenge: Different Topics, from Medicine to Food

Hsiao Nai-yen was very good at learning foreign languages from a young age. She believes that living in a country where the language is spoken is the most important part of learning. She studied in Rome for 8 months during college. She was surprised that her speaking skills improved much more than in three years of studying in Taiwan. While traveling in Europe, she saw many international meetings that needed a lot of interpreters. This made her want to do this job. So, after she finished university, she went to Italy to study more and become an interpreter.

Hsiao Nai-yen said, "The biggest challenge of being an interpreter is that the topics are always different. Today I might work at a dentist meeting, two days later a food show, and then a business meeting or a visit from a government group. The jobs are in many different areas." She thinks that changing between languages is not the hardest part. Understanding the special words and terms for each field is what truly shows her professional skill, like the saying, "one minute on stage means ten years of hard work off stage."

Hsiao Nai-yen explained that interpreting needs a lot of preparation. You need to know about different industries or the backgrounds of the two companies in a business talk. If it's a food show, you need to understand the special qualities of different foods and what the chef wants to express with each dish. She said, "I wouldn't call it difficult, but it's a challenge. This challenge helps me keep getting better."

Machines Don't Understand Human Jokes; AI Can't Fully Replace Interpreters

As artificial intelligence (AI) gets better, some people think that translation jobs will be the first to disappear. But Hsiao Nai-yen is not worried. She thinks that AI translation is mostly "direct word change." It is still hard for AI to truly understand the cultural meaning of words. This is especially true for Chinese, which does not have very strict grammar rules. The same sentence can have different meanings in different situations or places.

Hsiao Nai-yen said, "For example, when someone praises you, Chinese people often answer humbly, 'No, not really.' If a machine translates this directly as a negative sentence, the meaning would be very different." She added that choosing the right word when translating can make a big difference in important talks. A small mistake can cause a deal to succeed or fail.

She explained that interpreters must accurately say what both sides mean. But in difficult situations, like diplomatic talks, human judgment is needed. You must consider the tone of voice and the goals of your side in the discussion. "If people start to use aggressive words, you need to be very careful about how much you translate. If a mistake in translation causes a misunderstanding or makes the other side angry, the talk could break down."

Hsiao Nai-yen said that even professional interpreters need special training and a lot of experience to understand the mood in a room. AI cannot easily do these detailed tasks right now. Also, if AI makes a mistake, someone who knows the languages needs to fix it.

Interpreting Had a Good Ten Years, but After the Pandemic, Prices Dropped

The Chinese-Italian interpreting market has busy and slow times. It's usually slower in the summer or during Chinese New Year. Hsiao Nai-yen usually has 3 jobs a month, but sometimes she has 5 to 10. However, she feels sad that she worked during the "golden ten years" of interpreting. After the COVID-19 pandemic, many businesses started to focus on saving money. They would choose cheaper options or hire non-professional interpreters, not caring as much about quality.

In Italian companies, it's also common to hire friends or family. Hsiao Nai-yen said that if a company needs an interpreter, they often find a relative or friend who speaks English. But just speaking a language doesn't mean you can interpret. Interpreting has many small tricks that people who haven't learned the skills find hard to do.

Hsiao Nai-yen also noticed that after the pandemic, many companies and government offices would choose to save money over quality. They would pick interpreters with lower prices, not looking at their experience or background.

Hsiao Nai-yen said, "Translation is a very specific market, and we should respect everyone's choices. But I often get jobs where I have to fix mistakes because the results were not good, or the message was not given correctly. In the end, they still need a professional interpreter." She said that recently, she often gets these "clean-up" jobs. The client might even have to pay for an interpreter twice. So, she hopes that more people will understand and respect how professional interpreting is.
