Japan's 102-year-old sets new record for oldest person to climb Mount Fuji

A 102-year-old Japanese man with a serious heart problem has become the oldest person to climb Mount Fuji. But he doesn't think it's a big deal.

According to Agence France-Presse, Yukichi Akuzawa, born in 1923, climbs mountains almost every week to train. He finally reached the top of Japan's highest mountain.

He climbed Mount Fuji in early August, and Guinness World Records recognized his achievement.

Akuzawa told Agence France-Presse, "I am six years older this time than when I climbed it before." He climbed the 3,776-meter-high mountain when he was 96.

He said, "I've been many times and seen the view from the top. It's nothing special."

In January, Akuzawa fell while climbing near his home. He then got shingles and was in the hospital because his heart was failing. After that, he started to get ready to climb Mount Fuji.

His family worried about his health. But his 75-year-old daughter told Agence France-Presse that Akuzawa still wanted to climb. "He got better so quickly that the doctors couldn't believe it."

Akuzawa took three days to climb Mount Fuji. He stayed in mountain huts for two nights. But the high altitude almost made him give up.

Akuzawa's daughter said that with help from his companions, including his granddaughter, who is a nurse, he finally reached the top.

When asked if he wanted to climb Mount Fuji again, Akuzawa strongly said, "No."

Japan Political Party Poll: Sanseito Ranks First Among Opposition Parties

A new poll from Japanese media shows that the Sanseito party, which is only 5 years old, has 9.9% support. This is the second-highest after the ruling Liberal Democratic Party. Sanseito, which used the slogan "Japanese First" in the July upper house election, has the most support from people in their 30s and 40s.

Sankei Shimbun and Fuji News Network (FNN) did a joint poll on party support rates on August 23rd and 24th. It showed that the Liberal Democratic Party had 22.2% support, which was the highest.

After that, Sanseito had 9.9%, the Democratic Party for the People had 9.3%, the Constitutional Democratic Party had 5.2%, Komeito had 4.2%, Nippon Ishin no Kai had 4.0%, Reiwa Shinsengumi had 3.9%, the Communist Party had 2.8%, the Japan Conservative Party had 2.4%, the Future Party had 1.0%, and the Social Democratic Party had 0.3%.

Compared to the last poll in July, the Liberal Democratic Party gained 1.2 percentage points this time. Sanseito gained 0.1 percentage points. The Democratic Party for the People lost 4.6 percentage points, and the Constitutional Democratic Party lost 1.6 percentage points. The poll shows that support for the Democratic Party for the People went down, and support for Sanseito went up.

Let's look at the top three parties with the most support by age group. For people aged 18 to 29, the Democratic Party for the People had 22.1%, Sanseito had 10.6%, and Reiwa Shinsengumi had 6.2%. The Liberal Democratic Party only had 4.1%.

For people in their 30s, Sanseito had 18.9% support, the Democratic Party for the People had 16.9%, and the Liberal Democratic Party had 9.6%. For people in their 40s, Sanseito had 21.0% support, the Liberal Democratic Party had 15.9%, and the Democratic Party for the People had 11.5%.
