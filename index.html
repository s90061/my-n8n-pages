Chiayi City and Cultural General Association Invite Japan's "Emerald Knights" to Perform on December 21

Chiayi City is celebrating its 321st anniversary this year. At the end of the year, it will hold the "320+1 Chiayi City Urban Expo." The city is working with the General Association of Chinese Culture. On December 21, they will invite the "Emerald Knights" from Tokyo University of Agriculture Second High School Wind Orchestra to perform in Chiayi City.

Chiayi Mayor Huang Min-hui said in a press release today that the "320+1 Chiayi City Urban Expo" will be held at the end of the year. The city has always worked hard to promote international music exchanges. This time, working with the General Association of Chinese Culture, they can invite the "Emerald Knights," a top Japanese high school band, to perform. This shows the strong friendship between Taiwan and Japan. It also shows Chiayi City's cultural confidence and charm as it steps onto the international stage.

The Cultural Affairs Bureau said that the "Emerald Knights" are famous for their strong, disciplined, and passionate music. Their performance in Chiayi City for Taiwanese music fans is an important step in promoting cultural exchange between Taiwan and Japan. It is also an important stage for Chiayi City to show its musical charm to the world.

The Cultural Affairs Bureau mentioned that the Tokyo University of Agriculture Second High School Wind Orchestra, known as the "Emerald Knights," is one of Japan's most famous high school wind orchestras. It has over 170 members and has won many gold awards in various wind and marching band competitions in Japan for many years.

The Cultural Affairs Bureau stated that the "Emerald Knights" are well-known in Japan. They also actively take part in international cultural exchanges. In 2023, they came to Taiwan for the first time. They performed powerfully at the National Day Celebration and impressed all the audience. This year, on December 21, they will come to Chiayi City for the first time. They will perform on the stage of the 2025 Chiayi City International Band Festival concert hall. They will bring Japan's best youth music.

Taiwan Stock Market Reaches New High, Then Falls 130 Points Below 26800

Today, the Taiwan stock market started high with a lot of trading, but then went down. During the day, the index reached 27507.16 points, which was a new record. But later, many people started selling stocks. The index then fell from positive to negative. It went below 26800 points and closed at 26793.15 points. This was a drop of 130.27 points. The total value of shares traded was NT$683.169 billion, the most in over four months.

TSMC's stock price moved up and down. During the day, it went up to NT$1460, setting a new high price. But by the end of trading, its gains were smaller. It closed at NT$1425, up by NT$10. Its total market value was NT$36.95 trillion. This helped the main index by about 80 points. Other important and expensive stocks like Hon Hai, Delta Electronics, Quanta, ASPEED, and Alchip-KY all went down. The overall electronics sector index dropped by 0.63%.

Most traditional industry stocks also fell. The cable and glass industry indexes dropped by over 5%, showing they were weak. The plastic and electrical machinery industry indexes dropped by over 2%. China announced it will charge a "special port fee" for ships owned, operated, or flagged by the U.S. Because of this, shipping costs quickly increased. This led to strong gains for companies like Yang Ming and Wan Hai, whose stocks went up to their highest allowed daily limit. The shipping industry index rose by 3.92%, showing strong performance.

Premier Cho Jung-tai: No Solar Panels in Reservoirs if Water Quality Cannot Be Managed

Cleaning solar panels in Wushantou Reservoir has caused some concern. Premier Cho Jung-tai spoke in the Legislative Yuan today. He said that the government has watched reservoirs with solar panels for a long time. The water quality has not been affected. However, in the future, if the government cannot manage water quality problems, it will not build more solar panels. This is unless they can fully control the technology.

Recently, people have been talking about the solar panels on the water in Tainan's Wushantou Reservoir. Today, the Legislative Yuan continued its general policy questions. Legislator Huang Kuo-chang from the Taiwan People's Party said that he found public information showing that other reservoirs also have solar panels. These include Fengshan Reservoir, Yanshuipei Reservoir, Jinhu Reservoir, Neipuzi Reservoir, and Agongdian Reservoir. This has made people worried about their drinking water.

Huang Kuo-chang pointed out that the Democratic Progressive Party's website has a section to correct false information. It says that the solar panels in Wushantou are not toxic and will not pollute drinking water. But he asked if the government will continue to put solar panels in reservoirs or stop doing so.

Premier Cho Jung-tai replied that developing different kinds of green energy is a big government plan. He said the government has watched the solar panels in reservoirs for a long time, and the water quality has not been affected. But in the future, if the government cannot manage the problems with solar panels and water quality, it will not build more. This is only if they can fully control the technology and safely monitor it.

Huang Kuo-chang noted that in the past, laws did not require environmental impact assessments before installing solar panels. This led to solar panels being put on hillsides, protected areas, sea areas, and reservoirs. Currently, the Ministry of Environment is discussing whether to require these assessments. However, solar power companies are also pushing forward. For example, one company said in an August meeting that the second phase of floating solar panels for Wushantou Reservoir is being prepared. He asked if the government will allow this second phase to happen.

Minister of Economic Affairs Kung Ming-hsin said that local governments have not agreed, so this project is not happening right now.

Huang Kuo-chang asked if environmental impact assessments would be needed for solar panels over a certain size in national scenic areas or sensitive areas. Premier Cho Jung-tai answered that sensitive areas and scenic areas need government protection.
